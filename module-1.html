Chapter 01

The Machine That Conquered the World

Imagine you could give any question to the most powerful machine humanity has ever built and get an answer within seconds. Stock market predictions. Weather forecasts. Medical diagnoses. The layout for city infrastructure. Tomorrow's protein folding. Molecular design. Financial risk.

But here's the thing nobody tells you: the classical computer is fundamentally a counting machine. An extraordinarily fast, extraordinarily precise, extraordinarily miniaturised counting machine. But a counting machine nonetheless.

At its core, every classical computer — whether it's the supercomputer at a hedge fund processing millions of trades per second, or the chip in your phone — does exactly one thing. It manipulates binary bits. Zeros and ones. On and off.

Key Insight

A modern smartphone processor contains over 15 billion transistors — each one a tiny switch that can be either on or off. The entire digital world runs on the logic of on and off switches, just repeated billions of times, billions of times per second.

For seventy years, this architecture has been good enough — better than good enough. From the room-sized ENIAC of 1945, to the personal computers of the 1980s, to the cloud data centres of today, this binary logic has never met its match.

The results speak for themselves. The Apollo 11 guidance computer had 4,100 transistors. Your laptop has over 50 billion. We went from being able to calculate a moon landing to being able to simulate cities, predict weather systems, and train systems that understand language.

But something changed. Quietly at first, then with increasing urgency, the engineers and physicists building these machines started running into a problem they couldn't engineer their way around. A problem that doesn't have a solution within the architecture that built the modern world.

Moore's Law
Chapter 02

Moore's Law — The Promise and the Problem

In 1965, a young engineer named Gordon Moore made an observation that would shape the next six decades of technology. He noticed that the number of transistors that could fit on a microchip was doubling approximately every two years. This observation — later called Moore's Law — became the guiding principle of the tech industry.

Moore's Law, as it came to be known, was not a law of physics. It was an observation. An engineering target. A self-fulfilling prophecy that the entire technology industry aligned itself around for half a century.

Think about what that means compounded over half a century. A doubling every two years, over fifty years, is 25 doublings. 2 to the power of 25 is over 33 million. Computing power grew by a factor of 33 million in fifty years. No other technology has ever matched that growth rate.

Universe Analogy

If cars had followed Moore's Law the same way computers did since 1971, a car today would travel at 300,000 miles per second — the speed of light — and cost about a millionth of a cent.

But here's the problem nobody wanted to say out loud: you cannot make a transistor smaller than an atom.

And we are getting very, very close to that limit.

Modern transistors are now measured in nanometres. A nanometre is one billionth of a metre. The latest chips use transistors as small as 2 nanometres. A strand of human DNA is 2.5 nanometres. We are literally approaching the size of biology.

At that scale, the comfortable, predictable rules of classical physics — the rules that let engineers design reliable switches that are either on or off — start to break down. And in their place emerges something deeply strange.

Key Concept
The End of Moore's Law
Moore's Law is not ending because engineers aren't clever enough. It's ending because they've reached the boundary where classical physics stops working. Below approximately 5 nanometres, the quantum effects become so pronounced that transistors stop behaving as reliable binary switches.
The Physics Wall
Chapter 03

The Physics Wall — When Electrons Get Weird

To understand why miniaturisation hits a wall, you need to understand one of the strangest phenomena in all of physics: quantum tunnelling.

In your everyday classical world, if you throw a ball at a wall, it bounces back. It cannot pass through the wall — that would violate every intuition you have about how objects behave. Particles are objects. They hit things. They bounce.

And waves can do something balls cannot: they can tunnel through barriers.

Quantum Concept — Tunnelling

Quantum tunnelling is the phenomenon where a particle passes through a barrier that it classically shouldn't have enough energy to overcome. It's as if the electron "borrowed" energy from the uncertainty principle to slip through the barrier.

When your transistor is supposed to be switched off — a 0 — electrons should not be flowing. But when the barrier between the "off" state and the conducting channel is only a few atoms thick, electrons start tunnelling through it anyway. The transistor leaks. Your 0 becomes a 1. Your computation becomes noise.

In a chip with 28 billion transistors, even tiny error rates become catastrophic. And there's a second problem equally brutal: heat.

Every transistor that switches state dissipates energy as heat. Pack 28 billion transistors into a chip the size of your fingernail and switch them billions of times per second, and you generate enough heat to melt the silicon itself.

The Heat Problem

The world's data centres currently consume approximately 200–250 terawatt-hours of electricity per year — roughly 1% of global electricity consumption. As AI workloads grow exponentially, that percentage is climbing. The heat is literally one of the major constraints on further scaling.

So to summarise where we are: classical computers have been doubling in power every two years for fifty years. That progress is now physically constrained by the quantum behaviour of electrons at the atomic scale and by the thermal limits of silicon.

The architecture that built the modern world — the bit, the transistor, the binary switch — has run into a wall it cannot break through.

But here is where the story turns. Because the very quantum effects that are breaking classical computers — tunnelling, superposition, entanglement — are not just problems to be avoided. They are resources to be exploited. And that is where quantum computing begins.

Unsolvable Problems
Chapter 04

Problems Classical Computers Simply Cannot Solve

The physics wall is one problem. But there's a deeper issue that even a perfect classical computer — one that somehow ignored the laws of physics and kept shrinking forever — could never solve.

To understand why, consider a problem called the Travelling Salesman Problem. A salesman needs to visit 10 cities and return home, using the shortest possible route. How many possible routes are there? 3.6 million.

Now scale it up. 20 cities? Over 2.4 quintillion routes. 50 cities? More routes than there are atoms in the observable universe. A classical computer checking one billion routes per second would take longer than the age of the universe to solve the problem.

Why This Matters in the Real World

The Travelling Salesman Problem isn't just an academic puzzle. It's the mathematical structure underlying logistics routing (Amazon, FedEx, DHL), protein folding (drug design), financial portfolio optimisation (managing trillions in assets), and quantum chemistry simulation (materials science, batteries, fertiliser production).

This class of problem — where the number of possible solutions grows exponentially with the size of the problem — is called NP-hard. Classical computers cannot solve them because they scale too fast. But quantum computers can, in principle, explore an exponential number of solutions simultaneously.

There are three categories of problem where classical computing fundamentally struggles, and where quantum computing offers a genuine different approach:

Problem Class 1
Optimisation Problems
Finding the best solution from an astronomically large number of possibilities. Logistics, portfolio management, drug molecule design, supply chain routing. Classical computers cannot even enumerate the possibilities. Quantum computers can explore exponential search spaces in polynomial time.
Problem Class 2
Simulation of Quantum Systems
Simulating how molecules, chemicals, and materials behave at the quantum level. Drug discovery, materials science, battery design, fertiliser chemistry. A classical computer simulating quantum systems needs exponential resources. A quantum computer is a quantum system — simulation is native.
Problem Class 3
Cryptographic Problems
The security of almost all modern internet encryption — RSA, elliptic curve cryptography — relies on the fact that factoring very large numbers into primes is computationally intractable for classical computers. Quantum computers can factor these numbers in polynomial time using Shor's algorithm.

Notice something important about these three problem classes. They're not niche academic curiosities. They are the core computational challenges of medicine, finance, materials science, and cybersecurity. They are multibillion-dollar problems that affect how we design drugs, manage money, build new materials, and protect data.

The Quantum Door
Chapter 05

The Door That Opens — Why This Matters Now

We've established that classical computers are hitting a physical wall in miniaturisation, and that certain important classes of problems are fundamentally beyond their reach. But why does this matter right now? Why aren't we waiting for quantum computers to be perfected in another decade or two before worrying about it?

Three things have converged to make this moment genuinely different from the theoretical promises of previous decades.

First: the hardware has crossed a threshold. IBM has publicly demonstrated processors with over 1,000 qubits. Google has demonstrated quantum advantage on specific tasks. IonQ, Rigetti, and others have working systems. These aren't prototypes in labs anymore. Real quantum computers exist right now.

Right Now — You Can Access a Real Quantum Computer

IBM Quantum offers free cloud access to real quantum processors via their website. You can write quantum programs in Qiskit — IBM's open-source Python framework — and run them on actual quantum hardware. This isn't simulation. This isn't theory. You can do this today.

Second: the investment has become extraordinary. Governments and corporations are not treating quantum computing as a speculative bet — they're treating it as a national strategic priority. The US, China, EU, UK all have multi-billion-dollar quantum initiatives. Companies like JPMorgan, Goldman Sachs, and Merck are building internal quantum teams.

Third, and most urgently: the post-quantum cryptography threat is already active. This is the one that most people miss — and it's happening right now, not in ten years.

The Harvest Now, Decrypt Later Threat

Nation-state adversaries are right now collecting encrypted internet traffic — financial records, government communications, medical data — and storing it. They cannot decrypt it today. But they're storing it anyway, because they know that when quantum computers become powerful enough to break current encryption, they'll be able to decrypt everything they've collected.

This is why understanding quantum computing is not a five-year problem for your organisation. It's a problem that starts today — specifically around cryptographic infrastructure. And it affects every organisation that handles sensitive data.

The classical computer conquered the world by turning the on/off logic of binary switches into the engine of the information age. Quantum computing does something far stranger and far more powerful. It abandons the binary switch entirely and embraces the weirdness of quantum mechanics as a computational resource.

In Module 2, we go deep into exactly how. We'll explore what a qubit actually is, what superposition and entanglement really mean (beyond the buzzwords), and how these quantum properties enable quantum computers to solve problems that are classically unsolvable.

The wall that classical computers hit is not the end of the story. It's the beginning of a new one.

Module 1 — Key Takeaways
  • Classical computers process binary bits — 0s and 1s — and have followed Moore's Law (doubling in power every ~2 years) for 50 years.
  • Transistors are now so small (2–3nm) that quantum effects like electron tunnelling cause them to behave unpredictably — the physics wall is real and present.
  • Certain problem classes — optimisation, quantum simulation, and cryptography — are fundamentally intractable for classical computers regardless of speed.
  • Real quantum computers exist today and are accessible via the cloud — IBM, Google, QuEra, and others have working systems.
  • The post-quantum cryptography threat (harvest now, decrypt later) is active today — not a future concern.
  • Quantum computing doesn't just compute faster — it computes fundamentally differently, using quantum mechanical properties as computational resources.
